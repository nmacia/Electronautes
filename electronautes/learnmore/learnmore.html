TerMITes

TerMITes are small wireless devices that record data from sensors that can help us better understand our environments and human behavior. TerMITes support many types of sensing, and can be attached to objects in buildings such as doors, windows, drawers, cabinets, tables, and chairs to register object usage. TerMITes directly log on to the Internet via low-power WiFi for ease of connection and automatically upload to a server.

The data shown in the Electronautes application is recorded in the school approximately every 10 minutes, and is sent to MIT where scientists can use it to observe how the classrooms change over the course of days and months.

Sensors

Presence

Passive InfraRed sensor (PIR)

units: 0, 1 (Binary)

0 1

Infrared is a special “color” of light that is invisible to humans, but can be felt as warmth when it absorbed by the skin. Infrared can also be seen by a special “electronic eye” like the one on the TerMITes. Humans, as warm-blooded animals, convert much of the food we eat into energy to maintain a constant body temperature of around 37°C (98.6°F). Because this is usually much warmer than the objects around us, sensors that can see infrared can be used to detect when a warm body has entered or left a space.

The electronic eye used on the TerMITes is called a “Passive InfraRed” sensor, or PIR. It is “passive” because it does not produce infrared to reflect off objects in the environment. Rather, it looks for objects producing their own infrared heat, such as people or even household pets. The TerMITes register a response of 0 if no body movement is detected or 1 when a body is found to be moving.

Object Motion

Spring Vibration Sensor

units: 0, 1 (Binary)

The object motion sensors on the TerMITes use a very slender copper coil or spring that vibrates when the device is moved. This vibration causes the spring to wiggle back and forth and occasionally come into contact with a stiff copper pin. A small amount of electricity running through the spring travels through the copper pin upon contact, telling the TerMITe that the sensor has been moved. These object motion sensor were attached to doors, and register a value of 1 each time the door is moved.

Proximity

Infrared Range Sensor (IR)

units: 0-200 millimeters [mm]

The Proximity sensor used on the TerMITes works by shining an invisible InfraRed (IR) light source on an object, and measuring the brightness of the reflection using a small “electronic eye” that is sensitive to infrared light. As objects move farther away, the reflections become dimmer. As they move closer, it becomes brighter. The range sensor on the TerMITes has been calibrated to detect to objects between 1 and 200 millimeters from the eye. The same range sensor that is used on the TerMITes is also used on many mobile phones in order to turn off the screen when it is placed next to your ear.

Window

Accelerometer Tilt Sensor

units: 0-180 degrees of inclination (angle) [°] or [deg]

The window tilt sensor is based on a technology called an accelerometer. An accelerometer is a small device that is sensitive to changes in motion as well as the direction of the earth's gravitational pull. When the sensor is tilted, its orientation to the earth's gravity changes, and the TerMITe calculates the difference as an angle that reflects the window's new position. Accelerometers are found in many other devices, such as physical activity monitors, where a bracelet is used to count steps by tracking the rhythmic swinging of the arms while walking. Accelerometers are also used in mobile phones to detect the orientation of the phone and rotate the content of the screen so that the bottom is always oriented to the earth’s gravitational pull.

Door

Magnetic Reed Sensor

units: 0, 1 (Binary)

A magnetic reed sensor is made up of two thin pieces of metal encased in a small glass tube. The metal reeds are mounted so that they touch each other when exposed to the attractive force of a magnet. Otherwise, they are normally not touching each other. When the reeds make contact, a small amount of electricity travels through the sensor, and tells the TerMITe that the door has been closed. When the door is opened and the magnet is no longer close to the sensor, the reeds separate causing the electrical current to stop flowing through the TerMITe. This change is recorded by the TerMITe indicating that the door is now open.

Magnetic reed sensors are also used in many laptop computers to detect whether the display is open or closed. When the magnet in the screen is close enough to the sensor in the keyboard, the computer knows it can shut down because the laptop has been closed. Many computers will also wake up as soon as the laptop is opened and the reeds separate.

Temperature

Temperature Sensor

units: Degrees Celsius [°C]

Temperature sensors are found in many electronic items where it is important to know if the device is operating normally or overheating. Temperature sensors are also used in digital thermometers and thermostats that help keep buildings at a comfortable temperature. The temperature sensor on the TerMITes measures temperatures ranging from -40 to +85°C. People tend to feel most comfortable when the temperature inside a building is between 18° and 21°C, but this varies from person-to-person and day-to-day.

Humidity

Relative Humidity Sensor (RH)

units: Relative Humidity (RH) [%]

All air contains invisible particles of water, called water vapor. Humidity is the term we use to describe the amount of water vapor in the air. Sometimes, when this amount is high, you can see the water vapor, as is the case with clouds. The higher the temperature, the more water vapor can be held in the air. Scientists use the term “relative humidity” to describe how much water vapor is in the air, relative to how much can be held. This is reported as a percentage from 0-100%. When the relative humidity reaches 100%, the air can no longer hold water, and what is left over “precipitates” into water droplets, like rain.

Our bodies are very sensitive to the presence of water in the air, and a relative humidity range of 30-70% is important for human comfort. At lower values, our skin will feel rough and our eyes, nose, and throat may feel scratchy. At higher levels, it is more difficult to maintain a stable body temperature, as the air is unable to absorb the sweat produced when we exercise.

Pressure

Barometric Pressure Sensor

units: Pascal [Pa]
100000 Pa = 1 Bar ≃ 1 atm (1,01325)

You may not be able to feel it, but the air all around us contains weight. How heavy is air? The weight of the air varies from day to day based on temperature and moisture content as well as winds high up in the atmosphere. Air is also lighter up in the mountains like Andorra than it is at sea level like Barcelona. For this reason, a balloon filled with air at sea level would eventually pop if it were brought high enough into the mountains where the lighter air outside would allows the higher pressure air inside to keep expanding against the surface of the balloon.

Scientists use a tool called a barometer to measure air pressure. How does a barometer work? The barometric pressure sensor used on the TerMITes actually works like a tiny balloon in the example above, except that it’s made out of a very thin material that changes how well it conducts electricity as it expands and contracts. The TerMITes measure these electrical changes to figure out the barometric pressure of the air.

Pressure sensors like this one are used in many applications, including airplanes where the sensors are used to calculate and maintain a steady altitude during the flight.

CO2

Carbon Dioxide Sensor

units: parts per million of CO2 [ppm]

Carbon dioxide (CO2) is a colorless, odorless gas, that is all around us. Every time you breathe in air, your heart pumps blood into the walls of your lungs where it absorbs oxygen and releases carbon dioxide. As you breathe out, you release the carbon dioxide-rich air into the space around you. For this reason, CO2 sensors are a great way to help figure out how many people are in a room.

The CO2 sensors on the TerMITes take advantage of the fact that CO2 absorbs a small portion of an invisible “color” of light called infrared. Although we can’t see infrared with our own eyes, a special electronic eye in the CO2 sensor can, and it measures the difference between the amount of an infrared light produced by a small bulb, and the amount it received. The difference in this amount of infrared light can be used to calculate how much carbon dioxide was present in the air sample.

The CO2 measurement produced by the TerMITes is reported as “Parts Per Million” or PPM, which is usually around 400 ppm in fresh air. This means that for every 1,000,000 grams of air, about 400 of those would be made up of carbon dioxide. With a lot of people in one room, that number could grow to over 1000 ppm.

Light

Light Sensor

units: lux [lx]

The light sensor used by the TerMITes produces the value of “lux”, which is a measurement of the amount of light falling on a surface. 4 or 5 lux is about the amount of light on a city sidewalk at night when the streetlights are on. 50 or 60 lux is about how bright a typical home living room is at nighttime with the lights on. During the day, your desk at school might be 200 or 300 lux. Outside under direct sunlight, the lux value could be 50,000 or more.

The TerMITes calculate lux using a special material that converts the energy in light into electrical energy. The TerMITes measure this electricity and compute a lux measurement from it.

Because the material inside the light sensor can convert light into electricity, it is also used in solar energy panels. However, because the amount of energy produced by each sensor is so small, many of these must be combined together in a “solar array” in order for it to be useful in powering household appliances.
